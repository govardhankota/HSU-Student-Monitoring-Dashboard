Executive Summary

The HSU Student Monitoring Dashboard is a master’s-level analytics project designed to demonstrate how higher education institutions can leverage data to monitor student risk proactively. Many universities struggle to integrate academic, financial, and engagement signals into a single, actionable view for advisors and program coordinators. As a result, interventions are often reactive, fragmented, and implemented too late in the semester to strongly influence retention and success.

This project addresses that gap by constructing a synthetic dataset of 2,000 students that includes demographic identifiers, program information, academic performance indicators, fee payment status, outstanding balance, and a composite risk score. Clear business rules are used to ensure that, for example, students who have fully paid their tuition have an outstanding balance of zero, while students with balances above a threshold are flagged as higher financial risk. These data are modeled into a one-page Power BI dashboard that provides advisors with a concise view of high-risk students by program, risk band, and financial standing.

The resulting prototype illustrates how a student monitoring dashboard can support early identification of students who may need academic or financial assistance. While the dataset is synthetic, the structure, metrics, and interactions are designed to be realistic and transferable to a real institutional context.

Problem Context & Stakeholders

Problem Context

Student success initiatives often depend on fragmented data stored in multiple systems: learning management platforms, student information systems, and financial offices. Advisors may lack a consolidated, visual way to monitor which students are academically at risk, financially vulnerable, or disengaged. Without a unified view, institutions risk missing key intervention opportunities, particularly for students with multiple overlapping risk factors.

Stakeholders

  • Academic Advisors: Need prioritized lists of students by risk band and program.
  • Program Coordinators: Require aggregate views of risk across cohorts and majors.
  • Financial Aid & Bursar Offices: Monitor outstanding balances and payment risks.
  • Students (Indirectly): Benefit from earlier outreach and support aligned with their risk profile.

Data Design & Synthetic Dataset

Dataset Structure

The HSU dataset is fully synthetic and constructed to emulate a realistic student information environment.

  • ~2,000 rows of student-level data.
  • Core fields: Student_ID, Name, Program, Year Level, GPA Band.
  • Financial fields: Fees_Paid (Yes/No), Outstanding_Balance, Scholarship_Flag.
  • Risk fields: Academic_Risk_Score, Financial_Risk_Score, Overall_Risk_Band.

Business Rules

To keep the dataset coherent and analytically meaningful, a set of explicit rules was applied:

  • If Fees_Paid = "Yes", then Outstanding_Balance = 0.
  • Students with Outstanding_Balance > 100 receive elevated financial risk weighting.
  • Academic risk is influenced by GPA band, failed courses, and program year.
  • Overall risk band (Low / Medium / High) is derived from combined academic and financial scores.

Risk Model & Key Performance Indicators

Risk Scoring Model

  • Academic Risk Score: based on GPA band and failed course count.
  • Financial Risk Score: based on outstanding balance thresholds and fee payment status.
  • Overall Risk Band: categorizes students into Low, Medium, or High risk for quick prioritization.

Core KPIs

  • Number and percentage of students in each risk band.
  • Total and average outstanding balance by program.
  • Distribution of GPA bands among high-risk students.
  • Count of at-risk students per advisor or program area.

Dashboard Insights

Key Findings

  • A relatively small subset of students accounts for a large percentage of total outstanding balance, suggesting targeted financial outreach could be highly impactful.
  • Many high-risk students cluster in specific programs and GPA bands, providing a clear focus area for academic support initiatives.
  • Filtering by risk band and year level helps advisors quickly identify first-year or transition-year students who may benefit from more intensive advising.

Dashboard Experience

The one-page Power BI dashboard is designed for quick scanning and action-oriented decision-making. It combines KPI cards (e.g., total at-risk students, total outstanding balance) with segmented bar charts, risk distribution visuals, and tables of individual students.

Privacy & Ethical Considerations

  • Data Simulation: All records are synthetic and do not represent real students; the structure is inspired by realistic institutional scenarios only.
  • Responsible Use: A real deployment would need strong governance, role-based access controls, and adherence to regulations such as FERPA or equivalent privacy frameworks.
  • Interpretation: Risk scores should be used as starting points for conversation, not as labels that stigmatize students.
  • Bias Awareness: Institutions should regularly review risk models for unintended bias across demographic or program groups.

Lessons Learned & Next Steps

  • Designing a coherent student dataset requires clear rules that connect academic, financial, and risk fields in meaningful ways.
  • One-page dashboards are effective when they balance summary KPIs with enough detail to support action (e.g., lists of specific high-risk students).
  • Visualizing risk by program, year level, and GPA band provides a useful bridge between institutional strategy and advisor workflows.
  • Future work could incorporate attendance, LMS activity, and survey engagement metrics to strengthen the risk model.
